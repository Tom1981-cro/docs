The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. It sets out binding rules for how AI systems can be developed, placed on the market, and used across the European Union. Whether you build AI products, buy them from a vendor, or simply use an AI-powered tool in your day-to-day operations, this regulation almost certainly applies to you.

## What is the EU AI Act trying to do?

The Act takes a risk-based approach: the stricter the potential harm an AI system could cause, the heavier the compliance obligations. Systems that pose no meaningful risk are largely unaffected, while systems that could threaten fundamental rights, safety, or democratic processes face the most rigorous requirements — and in some cases are banned outright.

## Who does it apply to?

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Providers" icon="code">
    Any organisation or individual that develops an AI system and places it on the EU market or puts it into service in the EU — including companies based outside the EU whose systems are used by EU users.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Deployers" icon="building">
    Any organisation or individual that uses an AI system under their own authority as part of a professional activity — for example, a company using an AI-powered recruitment tool or a bank using an AI credit-scoring model.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Importers & Distributors" icon="truck">
    Businesses that bring third-party AI systems into the EU market or make them available to users carry their own set of obligations, particularly around ensuring provider compliance.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Non-EU Companies" icon="globe">
    If your AI system's output is used in the EU, you are in scope — regardless of where your company is headquartered. The Act has explicit extra-territorial reach.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Note>
  Even if you are a small or medium-sized enterprise, the EU AI Act applies to you. SME status does not exempt you from compliance — although certain procedural accommodations exist for very small providers.
</Note>

## The four risk tiers

The Act classifies every AI system into one of four risk categories. Your category determines which obligations you must meet.

| Tier | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Unacceptable Risk** | Banned outright | Social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces |
| **High Risk** | Full obligation set applies | AI in hiring, credit scoring, critical infrastructure management |
| **Limited Risk** | Transparency obligations only | Chatbots, AI-generated images or video |
| **Minimal Risk** | No mandatory obligations | Spam filters, AI in video games |

For a detailed breakdown of each tier, see the [Risk Tiers guide](/knowledge-base/risk-tiers).

## Key application dates

The Act does not switch on all at once — obligations have been phased in over several years.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="August 2024">
    **Regulation enters into force.** The legal text of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 became binding EU law on 1 August 2024.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="February 2025">
    **Prohibited practices and AI literacy apply.** From 2 February 2025, Article 5 (prohibited AI practices) and Article 4 (AI literacy duties) are in force. Any AI system that falls into a prohibited category must be shut down or redesigned immediately.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="August 2025">
    **GPAI obligations and penalties apply.** General-Purpose AI model obligations (Article 53), governance bodies, and the administrative penalty regime (Article 99) became active on 2 August 2025.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="December 2027">
    **High-risk Annex III stand-alone systems.** Full obligations for stand-alone high-risk AI systems listed in Annex III (as revised by the Digital Omnibus) apply from 2 December 2027.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="August 2028">
    **AI embedded in regulated products.** High-risk AI embedded in Annex I safety-regulated products (e.g. medical devices, machinery) faces its full obligation deadline on 2 August 2028 (as revised by the Digital Omnibus).
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Warning>
  The Digital Omnibus instrument revised the original August 2026 and August 2027 high-risk deadlines. Final adoption of the Digital Omnibus is still pending — confirm the operative dates with qualified legal counsel before planning your compliance roadmap.
</Warning>

## Penalties for non-compliance

The Act empowers national market-surveillance authorities to impose significant administrative fines:

- **Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher) for violations of the prohibited practices under Article 5.
- **Up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover** for most other obligations — including high-risk system duties.
- **Up to €7.5 million or 1% of global annual turnover** for providing incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information to authorities.

For SMEs, even the lower bands represent a material financial risk. The Act is already in force — preparation cannot wait.
